# Extraction Patterns

Instructions for identifying content-worthy moments in meeting transcripts and notes.

## What to Extract

### Hot Takes
Strong opinions stated with conviction. The speaker sounds certain, not hedging.
- "I think [X] is completely wrong because..."
- "Everyone does [Y] but that's actually backwards"
- "We've been measuring the wrong thing this whole time"

Signal: conviction, no qualifiers like "maybe" or "I guess." If they said it like they'd say it again, it's a hot take.

### Stories
Personal anecdotes with a beginning, a middle, and a point.
- "Last week we tried [X] and..."
- "I had a client once who..."
- "Three years ago I made this mistake..."

Signal: past tense, specific details (names, timeframes, numbers), ends with a lesson or outcome.

### Frameworks
Mental models or decision-making approaches that generalize beyond this meeting.
- "The way I think about it is..."
- "There are really only two types of..."
- "Every time I see [situation], I ask myself..."

Signal: the explanation would make sense to someone who wasn't in the meeting.

### Data and Stats
Specific numbers or surprising findings.
- "We found that 80% of..."
- "It takes the average person 6 months to..."
- "Our churn dropped 40% after..."

Signal: specific and surprising. Generic stats don't count—"AI is growing fast" is not extractable.

### Contrarian Views
Positions that go against what most people in the industry believe.
- "Everyone says [X] but actually..."
- "The conventional wisdom here is wrong"
- "I used to believe [X] until I saw..."

Signal: directly contradicts a common belief, and the speaker can back it up.

### Teaching Moments
Explanations that made something click for the other person in the meeting.
- "The simplest way to understand this is..."
- "Think of it like..."
- "Here's the analogy I use..."

Signal: the other person said "oh, that makes sense" or the explanation was unusually clear.

### Recurring Themes
Topics that came up in two or more meetings today. Frequency is signal—it's on the speaker's mind.

---

## What to Skip

- **Internal politics**: decisions, disputes, org dynamics that shouldn't be public
- **Confidential specifics**: client names without consent, revenue figures, legal matters
- **Logistics**: scheduling, next steps, action items—none of this is content
- **Small talk**: weather, sports, "how was your weekend"
- **Meeting-specific context**: things that only make sense if you were in the room
- **Anything the user would hesitate to say publicly**: when in doubt, ask

---

## Ranking Extracted Moments

When you have multiple candidates, rank by:
1. **Specificity** — concrete beats abstract every time
2. **Surprise** — would this change how an audience member thinks about something?
3. **Generalizability** — does it apply beyond the user's specific situation?
4. **Quotability** — could you put this in a subject line or tweet and it would stop someone?

Lead the interview with rank 1. Don't bury the best angle.
