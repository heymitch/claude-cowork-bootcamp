# Interview Questions

Templates for turning extracted moments into content angles. These are not a fixed list—adapt each question to the specific moment you extracted.

## The Core Pattern

Present the extracted moment, then ask one focused question. Never ask two questions at once.

> "[Paraphrase what they said]. Is that something your audience would find surprising?"
> "You said [quote] in your meeting with [person]. Want to turn that into a post?"
> "The story about [X]—could you tell that publicly? It would make a strong LinkedIn post."
> "You explained [concept] really clearly to [attendee]. Want to write that up for [platform]?"
> "[Topic] came up in two meetings today. Seems like it's on your mind—worth a post?"

---

## Question Templates by Extraction Type

### For Hot Takes
> "You had a strong take on [topic]: '[paraphrase].' Is that a position you'd want your audience to see? It's the kind of thing people either strongly agree or strongly disagree with—good content."

### For Stories
> "The story about [X]—is that one you could tell publicly? I can turn it into a [platform] post. The specifics are what make it land."

### For Frameworks
> "You explained [concept] in a way that would click for a lot of people. Want to write that up? It would work as a [LinkedIn post / newsletter section]."

### For Data/Stats
> "You mentioned [specific stat]. Is that something you can share publicly? That number would stop someone mid-scroll."

### For Contrarian Views
> "You pushed back on [conventional wisdom] in that meeting. That's a post. Who's the audience who needs to hear it?"

### For Teaching Moments
> "You used [analogy/explanation] to explain [concept]. That clarity is exactly what people pay for in a LinkedIn post. Can I draft that?"

### For Recurring Themes
> "[Topic] came up in both your [meeting A] and [meeting B] today. When something shows up twice in one day, it's usually something you're working through. Worth a post?"

---

## The One Wildcard Question

At the end, if nothing has landed yet—or as the fifth question:
> "What's the one thing from today's meetings that you'd want your audience to know? Doesn't have to be polished. Just say it."

This catches angles the extraction missed. The answer to this question is often the best post of the session.

---

## Rules for the Interview

**Max 5 questions per session.** Stop after 5 even if there are more angles. Overwhelm kills momentum.

**Lead with the strongest angle first.** If question 1 lands, you might only need question 2. Don't save the best for last.

**Accept "skip" or "no" immediately.** Move on without explanation or re-asking. The user knows their material.

**If the user says "that's internal":** Drop it completely. Never reference it again in this session or any future session.

**Watch for energy.** If the user expands on an answer—adds details, starts explaining unprompted—that's the post. Say: "That's the angle. Let me draft that now." Don't wait for formal approval to start writing.

**One follow-up is allowed.** If the user gives a vague answer ("yeah, maybe that could work"), ask one clarifying question: "Who's the audience for that take?" or "What's the surprising part they'd miss if you didn't write it?" Then move to drafting.
