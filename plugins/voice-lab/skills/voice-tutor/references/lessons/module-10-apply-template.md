# Module 10: How To Use Your Voice Template With AI

Now that you've completed all the voice personalization exercises, it's time to put your Voice Template to work.
You've got your unique writing DNA locked in, and now it's just a matter of instructing AI to write in *your* voice. This section will walk you through how to use your Voice Template with other prompts to create content that truly sounds like you.

## Step-by-Step Guide

1. **Start with your Voice Template**: Copy the Voice Template you created during the personalization exercises. This includes all your voice, tone, style, and structure attributes.
2. **Write a specific content prompt**: Clearly state what you want the AI to write. For example, if you're writing a tweet, blog post, or email, include the main topic and any important points you want covered.
3. **Add your Voice Template**: Attach the Voice Template to your prompt. This will guide the AI to write in your unique style.
4. **Run the prompt**: Input the full prompt (with the voice template) into your AI tool and let it generate the content.
5. **Review and refine**: The AI output should be pretty close to your voice, but it's always a good idea to review and make slight tweaks if necessary to ensure it matches your tone exactly.

We'll use the following The Pragmatic Problem Solver template from the last lesson as an example across three different types of content: short-form, long-form, and newsletters.

Here's the template again, so you don't have to click back to the last lesson to find it.

**The Pragmatic Problem Solver Voice Template:**

```javascript
Write in the style of a Pragmatic Problem Solver.

Use the following guidelines:

Voice Attributes:
- Direct
- Practical
- Analytical
- Solution-oriented
- Concise

Tone Attributes:
- Matter-of-fact
- Confident
- Humor Style: Occasional dry humor, but mostly straightforward

Style Attributes:
- Uses clear, step-by-step explanations
- Employs bullet points and numbered lists frequently
- Favors active voice
- Incorporates relevant examples and analogies

Vocabulary Preferences:
- Power Words: Effective, streamline, optimize, implement, actionable
- Avoid Words: Maybe, hopefully, possibly
- Replacement Words: 'Utilize' instead of 'use', 'crucial' instead of 'important'

Structure Attributes:
- Sentence Length: Primarily short (5-10 words) to medium (11-20 words) sentences
- Paragraph Length: 2-3 sentences max, often using single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis
- Signature Moves: Start each major point with an action verb, end articles with a clear call-to-action

Quirk Quotient:
- Use tool or construction metaphors for problem-solving concepts
- Occasionally reference personal problem-solving experiences
- Anecdote Frequency: Include a brief, relevant anecdote every 500 words

Additional Instructions:
- Address the reader directly as 'you'
- Always include a 'Bottom Line' or 'Key Takeaway' section at the end of each piece
- Anticipate potential obstacles and address them proactively
```

Now, let's look at 3 ways you can use your Voice Template for different types of content.

*Note: Each of the below prompts are not best practice prompts. I've intentionally kept them to 1-line so you can see how this process works. If you want to learn more about the art of prompt engineering, you can check out the [Prompt Engineering Playbook](https://promptengineeringplaybook.co/).*

## 1. Short-Form Content (e.g., Tweets, LinkedIn Posts)

Here's how to use the Voice Template to create a short-form post:

```javascript
Write a 280-character text about why beginning writers should write on social platforms liked LinkedIn (and not on a blog).

<VOICE>
{Insert Voice Template here}
</VOICE>

<TEMPLATE>
{What}
{Why}
{How}
{Takeaway}
</TEMPLATE>
```

**Example Output:**
Example Twitter/LinkedIn post demonstrating the voice template in action.

## 2. Long-Form Content (e.g., Blog Post)

For long-form content, the process is exactly the same—just paste your Voice Template into the prompt:

```javascript
Write a 500-word blog post about how to stay organized as an AI writer.

<VOICE>
{Insert Voice Template here}
</VOICE>

Output:
Please format the article as plain text, like a blog post.

Use # for h1 headers and ## for h2 subheaders.
```

**Example Output:**
Example blog post demonstrating the voice template applied to longer form content.

## 3. Newsletter Content

Newsletters can follow the same structure, with the Voice Template guiding the tone and style:

```javascript
Write a quick 100-150 word introduction to a newsletter writing email subject lines with AI..

<VOICE>
{Insert Voice Template here}
</VOICE>
```
