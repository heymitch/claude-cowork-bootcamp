# Voice Matching Rules

Writing in someone's voice means disappearing. If the reader thinks "this sounds like me," you did it right. If they think "this sounds like AI wrote it," you failed.

## The #1 Mistake

Samples are for extracting HOW someone thinks and writes — not WHAT they wrote about. If someone gives you a poem about dragons, you don't reference dragons in their newsletter. You study the poem's rhythm, its sentence structure, its level of abstraction, how it builds an argument.

**Exception:** If a sample contains a codified framework they've built (a named method, a repeatable model, a proprietary process), that's fair game — it's part of their intellectual toolkit. But a creative metaphor used once in one piece? That belongs to that piece. Don't beat it to death across everything you write for them.

You're learning their brain, not copying their words.

---

## Step 1: Read the Voice Profile

Open `config.md` and find the voice data — this may be labeled Voice Template, Voice DNA, Voice Samples, or Voice Profile depending on how training was done. Extract PATTERNS, not content:

- **How they think.** Do they build arguments linearly or use metaphor? Do they zoom out to big ideas or stay concrete? Do they ask questions or make declarations?
- **Sentence patterns.** Do they write long flowing sentences or short punchy ones? Do they use fragments? Do they start sentences with "And" or "But"?
- **Word choices.** What register do they write in? "Stuff" or "materials"? "Honestly" or "frankly"? "Wild" or "remarkable"?
- **Tone markers.** Are they sarcastic? Earnest? Self-deprecating? Confident? Do they swear?
- **Structural habits.** Do they use headers? Bullets? All paragraphs? One-liners between sections?

Do NOT extract topics, themes, imagery, or metaphors from the samples. Those belong to the original piece. Your job is to write NEW content that FEELS like the same person wrote it — not content that references or echoes what they already wrote.

---

## Step 2: Sentence Length Patterns

Bad AI writing has uniform sentence length — every sentence is 15-20 words, medium pace, medium complexity. Real humans vary wildly.

**Rules:**
- Mix short sentences (3-7 words) with long ones (20-30 words).
- After making a big point, follow with a short sentence. It lands harder.
- Start some paragraphs with a single short sentence. Then explain.
- Never write three sentences in a row that are the same length.

**Example — bad (uniform):**
> Writing a newsletter takes time and effort every week. Finding the right topic requires scanning your notes carefully. The outline should follow a clear structure for readers. Your voice needs to come through in every paragraph.

**Example — good (varied):**
> Writing a newsletter is hard. Not the typing part — that's fast. The hard part is finding the thing worth saying. The idea that's been bugging you all week, the one you keep bringing up in conversations. That's your newsletter. Everything else is filler.

---

## Step 3: Vocabulary Rules

Use the person's actual words. Not synonyms. Not "better" versions. Their words.

**Rules:**
- If they say "stuff," don't upgrade to "materials" or "resources."
- If they say "honestly," don't change it to "candidly" or "to be transparent."
- If they say "wild," don't write "remarkable" or "extraordinary."
- If they use casual contractions (don't, can't, won't), keep them. Don't formalize.
- If they never use a word, you shouldn't either. "Leverage," "utilize," "synergy" — if it's not in their samples, it's not in their voice.

**How to check:** After writing a paragraph, scan every word. Would this person actually say this word out loud? If not, swap it for one they would.

---

## Step 4: Tone Calibration

Tone lives on a spectrum. Plot the person on each of these scales based on their voice samples:

```
Casual ──────────────── Professional
Sarcastic ───────────── Earnest
Self-deprecating ────── Confident
Blunt ───────────────── Diplomatic
Playful ─────────────── Serious
Opinionated ─────────── Balanced
```

**Rules:**
- Match their position on each scale. If they're 70% casual / 30% professional, write that way.
- Sarcasm is the hardest to match. If their samples show sarcasm, use it sparingly. Too much sounds forced.
- Self-deprecation should come from real vulnerability, not false modesty. "I screwed this up" is different from "little old me couldn't possibly."
- If they're blunt, don't soften their takes. "This is wrong" shouldn't become "This might not be the best approach."

---

## Step 5: Paragraph Rhythm

**When to use one-liners (standalone single sentences as their own paragraph):**
- To land a punchline
- To create a beat before a shift in direction
- To emphasize a key insight
- After a longer explanatory paragraph

**When to write longer paragraphs (3-5 sentences):**
- Telling a story or setting a scene
- Explaining something technical or nuanced
- Building an argument with evidence

**Rules:**
- Never write five long paragraphs in a row. Break them up.
- Never write five one-liners in a row. It reads like a list of fortune cookies.
- The pattern should feel like breathing: expand, contract, expand, contract.

---

## Step 6: Signature Phrases

Signature phrases are STRUCTURAL tics — transitions, openers, and conversational habits that appear across MULTIPLE samples on DIFFERENT topics. Things like:

- "Here's the thing..."
- "Let me be real with you."
- "And look —"
- "The short version:"
- "Not even close."

These are patterns in how they CONNECT ideas, not what they write about. If a phrase only appears in one sample, it's content — not a signature.

**Rules:**
- A phrase must appear in 2+ samples on different topics to count as a signature. One occurrence = content, not voice.
- Use 1-2 signature phrases per newsletter. Not more — it becomes a parody.
- Place them where they'd naturally appear in conversation. Don't force them into the opening line.
- NEVER recycle one-off creative metaphors from samples. "Here be dragons" in a poem about exploration is CONTENT — don't echo it in a newsletter about scheduling tools.
- DO reuse codified frameworks — if they've built a named method or repeatable model (e.g., "The 4-Floor Framework"), that's part of their toolkit and fair to reference.

---

## Step 7: The Read-It-Aloud Test

After writing the draft, read it out loud in your head as if the person were saying it on a podcast.

- Does any sentence sound like a textbook? Rewrite it.
- Does any paragraph sound like a corporate email? Rewrite it.
- Does anything sound like something a professor would write? Rewrite it.
- Would the person actually say this to a friend over coffee? If not, rewrite it.

---

## Anti-AI Patterns (Never Use These)

These phrases scream "AI wrote this." Remove every single one.

**Banned openers:**
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "In the ever-evolving landscape of..."
- "As we navigate the complexities of..."
- "It's no secret that..."
- "In an age where..."

**Banned transitions:**
- "It's important to note that..."
- "Let's dive in..."
- "Without further ado..."
- "That being said..."
- "With that in mind..."

**Banned filler:**
- "game-changer"
- "landscape" (as in "the marketing landscape")
- "leverage" (as a verb)
- "unlock" (as in "unlock your potential")
- "elevate" (as in "elevate your brand")
- "streamline"
- "robust"
- "holistic"
- "cutting-edge"
- "empower"
- "delve" or "delve into"
- "tapestry"
- "at the end of the day"
- "it goes without saying"
- "the bottom line is"

**Banned structures:**
- Three-part parallel lists in every paragraph ("It's not just X, it's Y, and most importantly, Z")
- Ending with "And that's what it's all about" or "And that makes all the difference"
- Starting consecutive paragraphs with the same word

## Before/After Examples

### Example 1: Generic → Voice-matched (casual, blunt)
**Before:** "It's important to establish a consistent content creation schedule that aligns with your audience's expectations and your capacity to deliver quality material."

**After:** "Pick a schedule you can actually keep. Once a week is fine. Twice is great. Daily will burn you out in a month. Whatever you pick, don't miss."

### Example 2: Generic → Voice-matched (professional, confident)
**Before:** "Leveraging data-driven insights can help you make more informed decisions about your content strategy going forward."

**After:** "Look at your numbers. Your top 5 posts tell you exactly what your audience wants. Write more of that. Stop guessing."

### Example 3: Generic → Voice-matched (self-deprecating, funny)
**Before:** "Building a newsletter from scratch requires patience and dedication, but the rewards are worth the effort."

**After:** "I started my newsletter with 14 subscribers. Twelve of them were family. One was my barber. But I kept sending it every Tuesday like clockwork, because apparently I enjoy performing to empty rooms."

### Example 4: Generic → Voice-matched (earnest, storytelling)
**Before:** "Many entrepreneurs face the challenge of balancing work responsibilities with personal well-being, often leading to burnout."

**After:** "Last March, I worked 80 hours in a single week. Shipped a product. Landed two clients. Felt like a machine. Then I missed my daughter's school play and sat in my car in the parking lot wondering what I was doing."

### Example 5: Generic → Voice-matched (opinionated, direct)
**Before:** "There are many approaches to email marketing, and it's worth exploring different strategies to find what works best for your unique situation."

**After:** "Most email marketing advice is garbage. Send value, don't spam, segment your list — yeah, everyone knows that. Here's what actually moves the needle: write like a person. Not a brand. Not a funnel. A person."
