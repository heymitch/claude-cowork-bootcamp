# Subject Line Patterns

Your subject line is the only thing between your newsletter and the trash folder. These patterns are proven to get opens.

---

## 1. The Number

People click numbers. They promise a finite, scannable read.

**Pattern:** `[Number] [things/lessons/mistakes] about [topic]`

**Examples:**
- "7 things nobody tells you about pricing"
- "3 mistakes killing your cold emails"
- "5 lessons from my worst product launch"
- "11 tools I actually use every day"
- "4 signs your funnel is broken"
- "9 rules I follow for writing that gets shared"

**When to use:** Listicles, roundups, any post with discrete takeaways.
**Open-rate behavior:** High open, high skim. People expect a fast read.

---

## 2. The Question

Questions create an itch. The reader's brain starts answering before they decide to click — and then they click to see if they're right.

**Pattern:** `[Question that challenges an assumption]?`

**Examples:**
- "What if your marketing strategy is backwards?"
- "Are you building the wrong product?"
- "Why do most newsletters fail by issue 10?"
- "What's the real reason your audience isn't growing?"
- "Is your content actually helping anyone?"
- "What would you do with 10 extra hours a week?"

**When to use:** Essays, opinion pieces, anything where you're challenging conventional wisdom.
**Open-rate behavior:** High open when the question feels personal. Low open if it feels generic.

---

## 3. The Curiosity Gap

You reveal just enough to create tension, but not enough to satisfy it. The reader HAS to click.

**Pattern:** `I [did surprising thing]. [Tease the result.]`

**Examples:**
- "I almost quit my newsletter. Here's what changed."
- "I deleted my entire content calendar. Best decision I made."
- "I sent 500 cold emails. The results weren't what I expected."
- "I tried the viral productivity hack. It broke everything."
- "I asked ChatGPT to write my newsletter. Then I read it."
- "I lost my biggest client. It was the best thing that happened."

**When to use:** Personal stories, experiments, case studies. Needs a real story behind it.
**Open-rate behavior:** Highest open rates of any pattern. But readers feel cheated if the payoff is weak.

---

## 4. The Direct Promise

No games. Tell them exactly what they'll get. Works when the value is genuinely compelling.

**Pattern:** `How to [result] in [timeframe] (without [common obstacle])`

**Examples:**
- "How to write a week of content in 2 hours"
- "How to land your first 100 subscribers (without paid ads)"
- "How to double your email open rate this month"
- "How to build a landing page in 30 minutes"
- "How to price your services without undercharging"
- "How to get 5 referrals a month without asking for them"

**When to use:** Tactical, how-to content. The promise must be specific and deliverable.
**Open-rate behavior:** Steady, reliable opens. Not flashy, but trustworthy. Best for building long-term loyalty.

---

## 5. The Personal Story

Vulnerability sells. People are drawn to real human moments more than polished advice.

**Pattern:** `I [emotional/surprising personal detail]. [What I'd do differently.]`

**Examples:**
- "I lost $20K on a course launch. Here's what I'd change."
- "I burned out in 6 months. This is the system I built after."
- "I got fired from my first agency job. It made me a better marketer."
- "I charged $50/hour for 3 years. The switch to value pricing changed everything."
- "I published 200 posts before one went viral. Here's what finally worked."
- "I spent $5K on a coach who told me one thing. It was worth it."

**When to use:** Storytelling newsletters, lessons learned, reflection pieces.
**Open-rate behavior:** Very high open. Readers remember these. Great for building trust.

---

## 6. The Contrarian

Say the thing everyone's thinking but nobody's saying. Challenge a popular belief.

**Pattern:** `Stop [popular thing]. [Why it's not working.]`

**Examples:**
- "Stop posting every day. It's not working."
- "Stop building your email list. Do this instead."
- "Nobody cares about your morning routine"
- "The best marketers don't use social media"
- "Your content strategy is a waste of time"
- "Consistency is overrated. Here's what actually matters."

**When to use:** Opinion pieces, myth-busting, contrarian takes. You need to back it up in the body.
**Open-rate behavior:** High open, polarizing. Some people love it, some unsubscribe. That's fine.

---

## 7. The News Hook

Ride a trending topic. Connect something in the news to your audience's world.

**Pattern:** `[Current event] + what it means for [your audience]`

**Examples:**
- "OpenAI just changed everything. Here's what creators should do."
- "Google's new update kills 30% of blog traffic. What to do now."
- "The layoffs aren't stopping. How to recession-proof your business."
- "Apple just launched X. Here's why it matters for your workflow."
- "Instagram is dying. Where your audience is going next."
- "The TikTok ban is here. 3 moves to make this week."

**When to use:** When something big happens in your industry. Must be timely — publish within 48 hours.
**Open-rate behavior:** Highest opens when the news is fresh. Stale news = ignored.

---

## 8. The Irresistible Headline (Compositional)

Instead of picking one pattern, compose a subject line from up to 5 building blocks. The more pieces you include, the more compelling the line — but email subject lines max out at 9 words / 60 characters, so you compress.

**The 5 pieces:**

| Piece | What It Does | Examples |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **HOW MANY** (a number) | Promises a finite read | 6 tips, 7 reasons, 11 mistakes |
| **The WHO** (an audience) | Self-selects the reader | for project managers, for therapists, for founders |
| **The WHAT** (a noun) | Names the content type | tips, reasons, mistakes, lessons, examples, templates, case studies |
| **The WHY** (a reason/outcome) | Stakes the benefit | to get promoted, to not lose your job, to buy your first house |
| **TWIST THE KNIFE** (2-3 additional benefits/outcomes) | Stacks value | to attract your first 5 high-ticket clients and move into your dream office |

**How to use:** Start with HOW MANY + WHAT, then add WHO, WHY, or TWIST THE KNIFE until the line hits ~9 words. Not every line needs all 5 pieces — but the best ones have at least 3.

**Examples:**
- "7 mistakes killing your cold emails" (HOW MANY + WHAT + implied WHO)
- "5 steps to write your first viral thread (even with 0 followers)" (HOW MANY + WHAT + WHY + TWIST)
- "3 keys to land $10,000 consulting clients, from home, in your pajamas" (HOW MANY + WHAT + WHY + TWIST + TWIST)

## Preview Text: The "Whisper" Strategy

The preview text (sub-text line) is the grey sentence that shows next to the subject line in the inbox. Most writers ignore it. That's a mistake — it's a second subject line.

**The subject line's job:** Hook the reader (like a note to a friend).
**The preview text's job:** "Whisper." A parenthetical that reinforces the hook and sets expectations.

**4 Whisper Types:**

| Type | What It Does | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Trust Whisper** | Adds credibility | "Written by a Twitter creator with 100K followers" |
| **Obstacle Whisper** | Names what they WON'T need to do | "Without spending any money on ads" |
| **Benefit Whisper** | Stacks an extra benefit | "And how to solve it" |
| **Outcome Whisper** | Paints the end result | "And start earning $250,000 per year in sweatpants" |

**Rules:**
- Keep preview text under 90 characters (gets cut off on mobile)
- Don't repeat the subject line — complement it
- Pick the whisper type that fills the gap the subject line leaves. If the subject line is a curiosity gap, the whisper should add a trust or benefit element.

**Example pairings:**
- Subject: "Avoid 3 common Twitter pitfalls in minutes." → Whisper (Outcome): "And save months of misguided effort."
- Subject: "I lost my biggest client." → Whisper (Benefit): "Best thing that ever happened to my business."
- Subject: "Your Life Coach career path blueprint... in 1 email!" → Whisper (Trust): "From a coach who built a 7-figure practice."

**4 Proven Hook Archetypes for Subject Lines:**
1. Here's a ton of value for minimal time
2. Here's a ton of value for minimal cost
3. Here's how to solve your problem without much effort
4. Here's how to unlock this desirable outcome, instantly

Every strong subject line maps to one of these. If yours doesn't promise at least one, it's probably too vague.

## Anti-Patterns (Avoid These)

### Spam triggers (email filters catch these):
- ALL CAPS in subject lines
- Excessive punctuation: "Wait!!!! You NEED to see this!!!!"
- "Free", "Act now", "Limited time", "Click here" (especially combined)
- Emojis in every subject line (one occasionally is fine, five is spam)
- "Re:" or "Fwd:" when it's not a reply or forward

### Trust killers (readers learn to ignore you):
- Clickbait with no payoff: "You won't BELIEVE what happened" → weak story
- Vague promises: "This changes everything" → changes nothing
- Fake urgency: "Last chance!" every single week
- Misleading: subject says one thing, newsletter is about something else
- "I need to tell you something..." → just selling a product

### Boring defaults:
- "Weekly Update #47"
- "Newsletter - February 20, 2026"
- "Some thoughts on marketing"
- "A few things I wanted to share"

## Power Words (Proven to Increase Opens)

**Urgency:** now, today, this week, before, deadline, final
**Curiosity:** secret, hidden, surprising, unexpected, strange, weird
**Value:** free, proven, simple, quick, easy, step-by-step
**Emotion:** mistake, lesson, failure, breakthrough, struggle, honest
**Specificity:** exact, specific, precise, the real reason, actually
**Exclusivity:** only, rare, few people know, insider, behind-the-scenes
**Action:** stop, start, try, build, fix, avoid, steal (as in "steal this framework")

Use 1-2 power words per subject line. More than that feels desperate.

---

## Testing Rules

- **A/B test when you can.** Most email platforms let you test 2 subject lines on a small segment, then send the winner to everyone.
- **Track your winners.** Keep a list of your top 5 subject lines by open rate. Study what they have in common.
- **Length matters.** 6-10 words tends to perform best. Under 50 characters so it doesn't get cut off on mobile.
- **Preview text is the second subject line.** Don't waste it. Write it intentionally — it shows next to the subject in most inboxes.
