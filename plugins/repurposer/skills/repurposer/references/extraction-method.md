# Extraction Method: Long-Form to Short-Form

## The Core Principle

A newsletter is a container that clarifies thinking and generates material. Short-form content is not a separate project — it is the same ideas compressed. Every section of a newsletter contains at least one piece of short-form content. Your job is to find it, not write it from scratch.

## Step-by-Step Extraction Process

### Step 1: Scan for Structure Before Reading

Before reading the newsletter in full, scan for structural markers. These are the highest-yield extraction targets:

- **Numbered or bulleted lists** — almost always a ready-made note with a hook
- **Headers and subheaders** — often a complete one-liner or framing device
- **Before/after comparisons** — "wrong way vs. right way" contrast structures
- **Timelines or sequences** — ordered events that tell a story in compressed form
- **Bold statements or key sentences** — standalone claims that work as opening lines

### Step 2: Work Section by Section, Not Idea by Idea

Don't try to extract a single "best quote." Go section by section. Each section of a newsletter is a candidate for at least one note. A 1,000-word newsletter with five sections should yield at least five notes — often more.

### Step 3: Remove, Don't Rewrite

The default move is deletion, not composition. Paste the section you're working with. Then ask: what can I cut? Keep trimming until only the essential structure remains. The note should say the same thing as the original section but in the fewest possible words.

What to cut:
- Transition sentences ("Now that we've covered X...")
- Setup and warm-up copy ("I want to share something with you...")
- Redundant examples (keep only the clearest one)
- Explanations that are obvious from the structure itself

### Step 4: Fix the Hook, Keep the Structure

Once trimmed, the only thing that needs new writing is the opening line. Rewrite the first sentence as a strong hook if the original is weak. The rest stays close to the source.

### Step 5: Match Format to Platform

After extraction, format the raw output for the target platform:

- **Substack Notes**: Short, self-contained. Can be a single punchy paragraph, a brief list, or a one-liner with a follow-up line. No explicit CTA required — your profile handles the conversion.
- **LinkedIn**: Slightly longer. Opening line must be a pattern interrupt. Lists work well. Can include a soft CTA ("What would you add?").
- **Twitter/X**: One strong sentence, OR a tight list of 3–5 items, OR a thread structure if the newsletter section is rich enough for multi-tweet expansion.
- **Generic social hook**: The opening line alone, stripped of everything else — strong enough to stand as a standalone post or seed a caption.

## Rules for Good Notes vs. Bad Notes

**Good notes:**
- Start with the insight, not the setup
- Are native to the idea — don't require reading the newsletter to understand them
- Say one thing clearly, not three things loosely
- Work on their own without a "go read my newsletter" crutch
- Match the platform's native format (plain text for Substack, punchy opener for LinkedIn)

**Bad notes:**
- Start with "In my latest newsletter, I wrote about..."
- Tease without delivering ("You won't believe what I found...")
- Are too long to read in 10 seconds
- Require context the reader doesn't have
- Are just a link dump with no original text

## Volume Expectations

A typical newsletter should yield:
- 5 minimum — aim for this as a floor
- 10+ if it contains multiple lists, examples, or frameworks
- Each section = at least 1 note; each list item can become its own post if expanded

## The Repeat Permission

Readers do not remember every piece of content they've seen. Repeating a framework, principle, or insight is not laziness — it's consistency. Each time a new reader finds you, your library of notes is fresh to them.
